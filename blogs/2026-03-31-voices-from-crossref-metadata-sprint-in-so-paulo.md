<p>This year, we placed a spotlight on the Latin American community, hosting the second Crossref Metadata Sprint in São Paulo, Brazil from 4 - 6 March 2026. In our first tri-lingual event, we brought together 31 participants from Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador, and Mexico. Our goal was to foster community co-creation using the open scholarly metadata. The Sprint was an opportunity to pose questions, share ideas, collaborate on research, and propose innovative solutions that enhance the use of metadata in scholarly communication and beyond.</p>
<p>Read on for more details about the content of the Sprint, and the resulting projects. You can also <a href="https://crossref.zoom.us/webinar/register/WN_V8OxcNeMRQ-CSLZXUlgWYQ" target="_blank">register to join our Sprint Showcase</a> call on 22nd April to hear directly from the team about their creations.</p>
<p>We were excited to receive more than 100 expressions of interest for this sprint. We&rsquo;re excited to see the growing enthusiasm in this space and will find new ways to channel it (watch the space!). We sought to balance that interest with ensuring a productive size of the group for the sprint.
Our participants included librarians, researchers, grad students, developers, journal editors, and scholarly communications professionals. They engaged in rich, multilingual conversations about the different editorial practices across the region - dynamically jumping between Spanish, Portuguese, and English.</p>
<p>We coordinated our activities and ideas with the SciELO Brazil team, who also participated in the Sprint. SciELO is one of the most recognized scholarly organizations in the region and an important source of open scholarly content and metadata; articles, books, preprints, and datasets published in different languages.</p>

<p>Susan Collins, Luis Montilla, Isaac Farley, Jason Portenoy, and Leandro Contreras from Crossref acted as facilitators, providing general support, answering questions, and clarifying the ins and outs of Crossref tools and interfaces.</p>
<p>Using the key learning from our <a href="https://doi.org/10.64000/30m1m-e8477" target="_blank">first Metadata Sprint</a>, we created opportunities for the group to interact and get acquainted remotely, before the event, to help them hit the ground running and maximise the value of the time we spent together. It really paid off in terms of quality and progress of the sprint projects.</p>
<h2 id="projects-summary">Projects summary</h2>
<p>The projects developed also have their own mini stories. When we opened the call for expressions of interest, we invited individual pitches for projects that make creative use of Crossref metadata to answer questions and support solutions to problems faced by our communities locally. During the preparations to the event, participants refined their proposals and coalesced into project teams. This is the list of the projects they progressed during our Sprint:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Crossref Metadata Refiner:</strong> A tool that queries the Crossref API for any publisher prefix or ISSN and computes a Crossref Health Score for each DOI, crossing citation count with metadata completeness. The result is a prioritized action list for the member: fix the most-cited papers with the worst metadata first.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Improving Error Messages in Crossref Submission Details:</strong> A proposal for the redesign of the presentation of error messages in the Crossref Submission Details interface. Instead of showing only raw XML diagnostics, display structured and human-readable messages.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Scholarly Retractions and Corrections Tool:</strong> The development of a user-friendly form for Crossref members to fill in correction and retraction metadata.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Best practices for metadata journals in OJS, according Crossref schema:</strong> A guide that shows the correct completion of metadata fields in order to avoid errors already seen in everyday work, and facilitate the correct registration of metadata with the necessary quality to avoid errors in XML export.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Metrics for publishers using the Crossref API:</strong> A Power BI dashboard that summarises members’ metrics and indicators based on metadata retrieved from the REST API.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Metadata Integrity Check:</strong> A proposal for the implementation of a second stage of XML integrity verification after using the XML parser. This additional step would allow preliminary verification of the presence of mandatory metadata elements and encourage the inclusion of recommended metadata, such as institutional affiliations and references.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Integration OJS / OPS - Crossmark:</strong> This project advanced the development of a plugin for the current versions of OJS (3.4 and 3.5), so that when an article receives a correction, update, or new version after publication, each published version of the same work receives its own DOI, while maintaining structured relationships between these versions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Automated Detection of Reference Rot in Scholarly Web Citations:</strong> An automated evaluation pipeline to retrieve citations containing URLs, then traces redirect behavior, and records HTTP status codes and final page locations. It also extracts evidence from both the citation data and the webpage itself, including titles, creators, identifiers, and other metadata.</p>
</li>
</ul>
